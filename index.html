The Big Idea

Old drugs, new tricks

Developing a brand-new drug from scratch takes over a decade and costs billions of dollars. But what if an existing, already-approved drug could treat a disease it was never designed for? That idea is called drug repurposing — and it's a real, active area of medical research.

A famous example: Thalidomide was originally a sedative, later found to treat certain cancers. Aspirin was a painkiller, now also used to prevent heart attacks. This project builds a tiny version of the same kind of system used by companies like EveryCure — using biological data to find those hidden connections automatically.

How It Works

Three pieces of data, one big idea

Each disease is linked to the proteins or enzymes known to be involved in it.

Understanding the Score

What does the score mean?

The score is simply a count of how many biological targets a drug and a disease share. A higher score means more biological connections — which generally makes a candidate more interesting to researchers.

Score 1
One shared target — a weak but still notable connection.
Score 2
Two shared targets — a solid candidate worth a closer look.
Score 3+
Three or more — a strong biological case for repurposing.
Important context: In a real research system, scores would also account for how specific each target is, how well the drug binds to it, and what the scientific literature says. This system uses a simplified count as a starting point.
Research tool only. All matches shown on this page are computational suggestions based on shared biological targets. They are not medical advice and have not been verified by clinical trials. Real drug repurposing requires laboratory testing, peer review, and regulatory approval before any drug can be used to treat a new disease.
